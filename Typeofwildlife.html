Wild Life In Sri Lanka

Wild Life Attractions In Sri Lanka

Hikadduwa Marine National Park

Hikkaduwa National Park is one of three maritime national parks in Sri Lanka. The national park has a surrounding coral reef with a high degree of biodiversity. The location was declared as a wildlife sanctuary on May 18, 1979, and it was upgraded to a nature reserve with extended land size on August 14, 1988. As the number of visitors grows, the deterioration of the coral reef will intensify over the next 25 years. To alleviate the ecological effects, the reef was established as a national park on September 19, 2002.

Kumana National Park

This smaller and quieter reserve, formerly known as Yala East, is close to its much larger and more famous sibling, Yala National Park. It's an excellent site for birdwatching and safari expeditions. Kumana National Park is located on Sri Lanka's south east coast and is made up of wetlands and dry tropical forest, which are sustained by the park's 20 lagoons and tanks. Although there are fewer elephants, crocodiles, wild buffalo, and turtles than in Yala, there is still a high chance of seeing elephants, crocodiles, wild buffalo, and turtles. Leopards and about a dozen bears also live here, but seeing them is extremely rare. A 200-acre bird sanctuary on a rich mangrove swamp is one of Kumana's best attractions. Visitors can get a bird's eye view from watchtowers.

The 18,149-hectare Kumana National Park, in Sri Lanka's southeast corner, is a well-known eco-tourism attraction and bird sanctuary where a variety of species breed and roost. The eastern portion of Yala National Park is Kumana National Park. The 'Kumana Villu,' a 200-hectare natural swamp lake fed by the 'Kumbukkan Oya' through a half-mile-long narrow channel, is one of the park's most notable attractions. In May and June, many water birds lay their eggs in this mangrove wetland. Birds such as pelicans, painted storks, spoonbills, white ibis, herons, egrets, and small cormorants are frequently seen.

Udawalawe National Park

The Udawalawe National Park website is part of the Info Sri Lanka network, and it strives to benefit not only the fauna and flora of the Udawalawe Wildlife Sanctuary and National Park, but also the lives of the people who live in the surrounding areas. In addition, the website aims to inspire these individuals to protect and treat the Udawalawe National Park as if it were their own, so elevating the park to a more prestigious national asset of Sri Lanka.

The mugger crocodile, spotted and barking deer, sambar , water buffalo, wild deer , and jackal are among the other interesting animals found in Udawalawe National Park, which also has approximately 200 kinds of birds. From November to March, birdwatchers have the best opportunity of seeing spectacular raptors such the changeable hawk-eagle, serpent eagle, and grey-headed fish eagle.

Bundala National Park

Bundala National Park is a 20-kilometer length of Sri Lanka's south coast that include five tiny lagoons. The reserve contains a diverse range of habitats, including scrub jungle, salt pans, sand dunes, and wetlands, allowing for a diverse assortment of fauna. The park's beaches are home to all five turtle species that lay their eggs here. Bundala is one of Sri Lanka's best birdwatching spots.

The reserve's lagoons, which are known for their outstanding migratory bird displays, also attract an astounding variety of water birds. The pink greater flamingo is a well-known visitor to the park, and large flocks of flamingos travel here from Northern India. Pelicans, ibis, painted storks, and spoonbills are among the other aquatic birds. The pink greater flamingo is one of the park's most well-known visitors, with large flocks of flamingos migrating here from Northern India. Pelicans, ibis, painted storks, and spoonbills are among the other waterbirds. A variety of unique birds, including the exquisite peafowl, can also be seen. Visitors can expect to see ecstatic groups of grey langur monkeys, crocodiles basking, and wild pigs foraging among the other species.
